Overview

What if point-of-sale systems worked… like magic?

This project explores how Brown Dining Services workers interact with point-of-sale (POS) systems through interviews, interface sketches, user personas, and journey mapping to offer data-driven improvement recommendations.

Problem

Outdated UI

POS systems are outdated and need a refresh

System Reliability

Outages led to long wait times, causing frustration for students and workers

Stressful Rush Periods

Rush periods are stressful and system reliability issues make this worse.

Research & Insights

User Interviews

From a series of semi-structured interviews, I gained the following insights:

  1. Dining workers appreciate the lack of menu nesting, as 2-click transactions make rush periods more efficient
  2. Dining workers have significant demands placed on their attention like answering student questions, providing take-out boxes, and standing for hours in distracting sensory environments.
  3. Dining workers find the UI to be simple, regardless of whether they perceive themselves as tech-savvy.
  4. System outages cause delays and increase stress during busy periods.

Accessibility & Inclusion

The design was focused on simplicity and ease of use, ensuring accessibility for workers with varying levels of tech-savviness. The large, colorful buttons and straightforward interactions made the system intuitive, even for those who might not be familiar with technology.

Solution

Design Recommendations

Based on the the user and design research, I developed the following design recommendations.

  1. Improved back-end reliability to reduce stress during rush hour
  2. Simplifying redundant menu options for dining halls with more options
  3. Auto-prioritize most commonly ordered meals to reduce cognitive load over long shifts
