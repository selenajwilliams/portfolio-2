Overview

What if you could filter dropdown menus for faster keyboard-based navigation?

In this accessibility-focused redesign, I designed a new search-based filter feature to navigate dropdown menus more efficiently via keyboard controls and reduce cognitive overload.

Problem

Not Accessible

The internet is not optimized for people who use screen readers

Slow Navigation

People who use screen readers experience slower navigation of drop downs

Cognitive Overload

Large dropdown menus can increase mental effort and reduce usability

Research & Insights

Exploring The Dropdown Accessibility Landscape

Mapping Out Digital Interactions Through State Diagrams

Impact

Delays

Without a filtering option, people who use screen readers have to wait a long time to find the dropdown option they are looking for.

Reduced Learnability

Reduced learnability, intuition, confidence occur due to non-standardized user interaction norms for dropdown menus.

Cognitive Overload

Large dropdown menus lead to the “burden of choice” requiring excessive mental effort and cognitive overload.

Design Process

Empathize and Define

To center the experience of screen reader users, I began by exploring how dropdown menus are typically structured and navigated.

Ideate

After identifying key friction points—especially the lack of filtering and non-standard interaction models—I brainstormed potential interventions that could support faster, more intuitive navigation. I focused on ideas that required minimal additional user training and could work across various screen reader tools.

Design

I designed a keyboard-accessible, search-based filter to easily and efficiently navigate drop downs.

Solution

Filtering functionality can enable more efficient navigation for screen-reader users and reduce cognitive overwhelm

Learnings

Designing for Edge Cases Benefits Everyone

Solving for screen reader users led to a cleaner, more intuitive interface for all users—proving the value of inclusive design beyond compliance.

Accessibility Is a Process, Not a Checklist

Accessibility isn’t just about adding ARIA tags at the end. It requires deeply understanding how people with different abilities interact with your product—and designing from their perspective from the start.

Empathy + Technical Precision = Good UX

Designing for screen reader users forced me to think in flows, semantics, and feedback loops rather than just visuals. It strengthened both my empathy and my attention to technical detail.
